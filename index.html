Seminar preparation

This week we will be holding a formal debate. A formal debate involves two sides: one supporting a resolution and one opposing it. This week you will be debating the resolution: There is no such thing as Green Data. One half of the Seminar group will be in support of this resolution, the other half will be opposing this. Each of you has been assigned to either one of the sides: [Link]

You are asked, as a group, to prepare arguments in advance. This does mean that if your group is assigned to support the resolution, you will identify arguments to convince the other party that "There is no such thing as Green Data". Of course, you would be advised to simultaneously think about arguments against the resolution that the opposing team might use against you. Think about statistics, case studies, and examples that you can use to support your argument or to counteract the other team's arguments.

The Seminar itself will be structured as follows:

  1. Both teams have 10 minutes each to finalise their arguments and plan for the debate.
  2. The first speaker on the supporting team presents arguments in support of the resolution (3 mins).
  3. The first speaker on the opposing team presents arguments opposing the resolution (3 mins).
  4. The second speaker on the supporting team presents further arguments in support of the resolution, identifies areas of conflict, and answers questions that may have been raised by the opposition speaker (3 mins).
  5. The second speaker on the opposing team presents further arguments against the resolution, identifies further areas. of conflict, and answers questions that may have been raised by the previous supporting speaker (3 mins).
  6. Both team have 5 minutes to prepare for the rebuttal.
  7. The opposing team begins with the rebuttal, attempting to defend the opposing arguments and to defeat the supporting arguments without adding any new information (3 mins).
  8. The supporting team follows with their rebuttal, attempting to defend the supporting arguments and to defeat the opposing arguments without adding any new information (3 mins).
  9. Each team gets a second rebuttal for closing statements with the supporting team having the last opportunity to speak (2 mins each).

Some further notes:

  1. All speakers are timed by the Seminar leader, you are not allowed to run over time and will be cut off if you do.
  2. The first two rounds is when you lay out your arguments and introduce your evidence in favour or against the resolution; no new information or evidence should be introduced in the final two rounds.
  3. There cannot be any interruptions. Speakers must wait their turns.
  4. Every speaker may only speak once.
  5. Have a little fun at the last class before reading week.
